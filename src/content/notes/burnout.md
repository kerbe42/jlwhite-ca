I burn out once or twice a year, and I have for years. It used to feel like something going wrong. Now it shows up like a familiar visitor: hello, old friend, what did I overdo this time? I know the face, I know the timing, and I usually know what tipped it. It doesn't blindside me anymore.

The shape is always the same. Things start to feel impossible. The clear path forward I can usually see just isn't there, and everything in front of me reads as one more stressful thing to handle. Progress slows down, at work and at home both. I slide into default mode: more brain breaks than usual, long video-game sessions, a show I binge instead of sleeping, the doomscroll. If [seeing the horizon and the path forward](/notes/imposter-syndrome) is who I am on a good day, burnout is the day the lights go off.

What took me years to land on is that burnout isn't a defect. It's a temperature check, and it's telling me I'm well done. It's a safety mechanism, the part of me that decides I need a break and then takes one whether I scheduled it or not. I pushed too hard on something, ignored the gauge, and the system tripped the breaker so I couldn't keep running it. That isn't weakness. That's the body and the mind doing what they're supposed to do when you run them too hot for too long.

Getting out is simple to describe and hard to do. It's a willpower check, and it's on you to pass it. Nobody hands you the way out. You muster the first move and force yourself to start going in the right direction. I reach for the quick, easy wins, the things right in front of me that can change. Put one thing away on the desk. Then everything else on the desk. The point of the first tiny win isn't the win, it's the momentum. You compound a small one into a bigger one, start the snowball and roll it into a snow boulder, and somewhere in there the path forward shows back up.

The brain breaks and the binges aren't the cure. They're the slide. The way back is the opposite of sinking further into the couch, and not all of it is grinding your way out by force. A lot of it is going through the motions: a walk, touching grass, putting your hands on something real. The garden does the most for me. There's a particular lift in watching a plant come back and thrive after I'd already failed it once, almost as good as the willpower itself. It lightens something. Growth and positivity turn out to be contagious, even when the thing growing is a seedling and not me, and especially then.

Understanding all this helps. I move through the cycle faster than I used to, waste less time arguing with the fact of it, and I read the gauge sooner now, before it pins. But understanding doesn't exempt me from it. It still comes, once or twice a year, and it's still a cycle I have to walk all the way through. The trick was never avoiding burnout. It's recognizing the old friend at the door, asking what I overcooked this time, and starting the snowball.
